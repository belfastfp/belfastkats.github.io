Jessica Kerr

@jessitron

 Rug: an External DSL for Coding Code Transformations (with Scala Parser-Combinators)

Programmers are automators. That's what makes us so valuable: a little bit of our work plus computers equals a lot of work done. Sometimes we automate our own work: scripts, IDEs, compilers, continuous integration. Can we take this further? how many of our coding tasks can be automated -- without adding indirection to the code?

Jessica will show a language designed for coding these code transformations, and the special Scala parser combinators that make manipulating Elm code extra expressive. You too may be inspired to write code that writes code, whenever writing code gets a little too easy.

About Jessica

Writes code at Stripe and speaks at software conferences around the world. She recently spoke at GOTO Chicago (May 2016), CodeMesh in London (Nov 2016) and YOW Australia (Dec 2016).

Bodil Stokke

@bodil

 The Realist’s Guide to Language Design

There’s a bewildering amount of programming language options growing in popularity these days. Instead of pushing one more in front of your face, asking you to fall in love with its shininess based on a limited set of demoable features, I’d like to take a step back and examine some popular languages from a language designer’s perspective. What benefits does this language offer? Why was it designed in this way? Which problems would this language be particularly suited to tackle? What would it be like working with this language day to day?


There’s no such thing as a silver bullet when it comes to language design - I’d like to show you, most of all, how to apply a critical eye when evaluating new—and old—programming languages

About Bodil

Born into an aristocratic Russian-German family, Bodil traveled widely around the Soviet Union as a child. Largely self-educated, she developed an interest in computer science during her teenage years. According to her later claims, in 1989 she embarked on a series of world travels, visiting Europe, the Americas, and India. She alleged that during this period she encountered a group of mathematical adepts, the “Haskell Language and Library Committee,” who sent her to Glasgow, Scotland, where they trained her to develop her powers of category theory. Both contemporary critics and later biographers have argued that some or all of these foreign visits were fictitious, and that she spent this period writing JavaScript.


Bodil was a controversial figure during her lifetime, championed by supporters as an enlightened guru and derided as a fraudulent charlatan by critics. Her doctrines influenced the spread of Homotopy Type Theory in the West as well as the development of Western computer science currents like dependent types, blockchains and isomorphic JavaScript.

Felienne

@Felienne

  Program Derivation for Functional Programs

Despite what your boss thinks, programs don’t just appear straight out of specifications. But…what if they did?

In this session I will show you how to systematically and step-by-step derive a program from a specification. Functional languages especially are very suited to derive programs for, as they are close to the mathematical notation used for proofs.

You will be surprised to know that you already know and apply many techniques for derivation, like Introduce Parameter as supported by Resharper. Did you know that is actually program derivation technique called generalization?

Nada Amin

@nadamin

  The cake and eating it: or writing expressive high-level programs that generate fast low-level code at runtime

In this talk, I will illustrate the promise of generative programming -- writing expressive high-level programs that generate fast low-level code at runtime -- with a small (‹500 lines of code) SQL query engine that outperforms commercial and open source database systems. The key ingredient is a naive interpreter for relational algebra operations, which we turn into a fast compiler with the help of the Scala type system. I will build the SQL engine step-by-step, focusing on generative programming patterns. Finally, I will touch upon verifying properties (e.g. no memory nor overflow errors, checked contracts) of the low-level generated code, so that it is not only fast but also secure using a high-level, fast and safe HTTP parser as an example.

About Nada

Dr Nada Amin is a member of the Scala team at EPFL, where she studies type systems and hacks on programming languages. She has contributed to Clojure's core.logic and Google's Closure compiler. She's loved helping others learn to program ever since tutoring SICP as an undergraduate lab assistant at MIT.

William Byrd

@webyrd

  Relational programming

Functional programmers write definitions that model mathematical functions. These functions clearly distinguish between input arguments and output values. In contrast, relational programming treats programs as mathematical relations, eliminating the distinction between input and output. miniKanren is an embedded domain specific language for constraint logic programming, designed for writing interpreters, type inferencers, theorem provers, and other interesting programs as relations. We will explore the extremely interesting and surprising behavior of these relational programs, and consider how this approach to programming might be made more practical in the future.

About Will

William E. Byrd is a Research Associate in the School of Computing at the University of Utah. He is co-author of 'The Reasoned Schemer', and co-designer of the miniKanren relational programming language. He loves cats, functional and relational programming, and the StarCrafts.

 Working hard to keep things lazy

When coming to Haskell from another programming language there are a lot of things that appear to be quite odd at first glance. One of these things is the non-strict evaluation strategy. Dreaded by some and loved by others, this might be one of the most controversial features of this programming language.

In this talk I will shed some light on the why and how of laziness and how GHC achieves it, as well as some interesting optimization techniques that the compiler performs to reduce some of the overhead that is introduced by it

Oskar Wickström

@owickstrom

 The Power of Functional Programming and Static Type Systems in Server-Side Web Applications

Single-page web applications have been spreading like wildfire, with an endless amount of frameworks and libraries, and functional programming together with static types fueling the fire with great ideas like pure functions, monads, and strong type checking. But what happened to Progressive Enhancement? Some parts of our applications might require Javascript to function, but the majority could be built with ordinary links and forms.

In this talk we will explore how we can build web applications using established web technology, and the power of functional programming and PureScript on the server-side, with strong correctness guarantees.

Edwin Brady

@edwinbrady

 Total Functional Programming

Idris is a functional programming language with dependent types, which supports "total" functional programming. A function is total if, for all well typed inputs, it gives either a complete result or a finite prefix of an infinite result, in finite time. Total functions give us strong guarantees about their behaviour: for example functions will never crash due to badly formed inputs, and servers will always produce responses to requests.

In this talk I'll show how Idris supports total programming, with a series of examples. As an extended example to show how total programming works in practice, I'll show how to define a type for describing communicating concurrent systems which, by writing total functions, guarantees that concurrent programs will interact as intended.

About Edwin

Edwin is a Lecturer in Computer Science at the University of St Andrews, interested in type theory, dependently typed functional programming, compilers and domain specific languages (DSLs). He is currently working on the implementation of DSLs for stateful, resource-aware programming, especially for correct network protocol design and implementation, using Idris, a dependently typed functional programming language. You might find him playing Go (I’m about 1 kyu), walking up a hill, watching a game of cricket, or waiting for a delayed train(this being his favourite). He also created the whitespace programming language.

 Introduction to Functional Programming in Scala with Cats

 Zalando - 3 Grand Canal Quay, Grand Canal Dock, Dublin (map)

Scala is a hybrid functional/object oriented language. The "cats" library focuses on the first part: functional programming. Besides the "usual suspects" like functors and monads it also brings useful data that can be used to improve code correctness.

In this tutorial, you'll learn how "type classes" work in Scala and write some simple, generic programs. We'll also have a look at some of the useful monads cats has to offer. Finally, we'll put it all together to write a simple web service that is very easy to test. Time permitting, we can also look at how type class laws help us to spot errors in our code.

Nada Amin and William Byrd

 Thinking About Recursion

 Workday - Workday, Kings Building, May Lane, Dublin 7 (map)

Recursion is everywhere in programming and programming languages. For example, recursion is the natural way to express computation over trees. In this tutorial we will learn how to think about recursion, and practice the natural way to write recursive definitions. Topics will include: natural recursion and tail recursion; recursion over trees and nested data structures; mutual recursion; tail call optimization; using program transformations to avoid blowing the stack in languages that do not support tail calls properly; recursion and induction; corecursion, coinduction, and streams; recursive macros; interesting uses of recursion, such as representing state machines as mutually-recursive higher-order functions, and a meta-circular Lisp interpreter.

Code Of Conduct

With Kats Conf 2, we want to bring the Irish programming community together for a conference about the fun and awesomeness of functional programming.

We value the participation of each and every member of our community. We want everyone to feel welcome and have an enjoyable experience, beginners and experts alike, regardless of who they are or whether they have any previous knowledge of functional programming.

All attendees, speakers, organisers, volunteers and sponsors are expected to follow this Code of Conduct at conference and workshop venues and conference-related social events. Organisers will enforce this code throughout the event.

Why have a code of conduct? Not because we were asked to, not because we were expected to have one and pasted one from another conference website. But as part of an intentional effort to define the culture of Kats Conf.

The quick version

Our conference is dedicated to providing a harassment-free conference experience for everyone, regardless of gender, gender identity and expression, age, sexual orientation, disability, physical appearance, body size, race, ethnicity, or religion (or lack thereof). We do not tolerate harassment of conference participants in any form. Sexual language and imagery is not appropriate for any conference venue, including talks, workshops, parties, Twitter and other online media. Conference participants violating these rules may be sanctioned or expelled from the conference without a refund at the discretion of the conference organisers.

The less quick version

Harassment includes offensive verbal comments related to gender, gender identity and expression, age, sexual orientation, disability, physical appearance, body size, race, ethnicity, religion, sexual images in public spaces, deliberate intimidation, stalking, following, harassing photography or recording, sustained disruption of talks or other events, inappropriate physical contact, and unwelcome sexual attention.

Participants asked to stop any harassing behavior are expected to comply immediately.

Sponsors are also subject to the anti-harassment policy. In particular, sponsors should not use sexualised images, activities, or other material. Booth staff (including volunteers) should not use sexualised clothing/uniforms/costumes, or otherwise create a sexualised environment.

If a participant engages in harassing behaviour, the conference organisers may take any action they deem appropriate, including warning the offender or expulsion from the conference with no refund.

Social rules

In addition to the above anti-harassment rules, we'd like to experiment asking people to follow a small set of social rules. Unlike the rest of the code of conduct, breaking these rules won't result in a warning/exclusion from the conference or anything of the like.

These rules originate from the Recurse Center, and have been adopted among others by the Scala contributors Code of Conduct. They basically boil down to "don't be a jerk", but of course, nobody sets out to be a jerk, so saying this isn't really productive. Instead, here are a few specific behaviours we encourage you to avoid when engaging other people during the conference:

  • providing corrections that do not improve the conversation (sometimes referred to as "well actually"s)
  • repeatedly interrupting or talking over someone else
  • feigning surprise at someone’s lack of knowledge or awareness about a topic ("What? You don't know what an Applicative Functor is!!?")
  • subtle prejudice (for example, comments like "That’s so easy your mother could do it.")

The world of functional programming can be intimidating (and full of complicated jargon). We hope to alleviate this by encouraging people to follow these rules.

Contact information

If you are being harassed, notice that someone else is being harassed, or have any other concerns, please contact a member of conference staff. Conference staff can be identified as they'll be wearing branded hoodies.

If the matter is especially urgent, call or text Jérémie Jost, at: +383 83 824 1049

You can also send us an email, at katsconf+report@gmail.com, or use this form to report a violation, with or without your name/contact details.

If required, conference staff will be happy to help participants contact venue security or local law enforcement, provide escorts, or otherwise assist those experiencing harassment to feel safe for the duration of the conference. We value your attendance.
